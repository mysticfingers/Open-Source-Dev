The Wisdom of Insecurity

The Age of Anxiety by Alan Watts is a reflection on the modern world's struggle with existential issues. Here are the main points:

●        Modern Dilemma: Focuses on the anxiety and uncertainty people face in the contemporary world, driven by rapid technological progress, social change, and the loss of traditional beliefs.

●        Loss of Meaning: Watts argues that people feel disconnected from a sense of purpose or meaning in life, as old religious and cultural structures no longer provide a foundation.

●        Fear of the Unknown: Explores how modern individuals often feel lost, anxious, and afraid of the future, leading to existential dread.

●        Materialism vs. Spirituality: Suggests that an overemphasis on materialism and scientific rationalism contributes to a sense of alienation, neglecting spiritual or deeper connections to life.

●        Balance: Advocates for finding a balance between the material and the spiritual, embracing uncertainty, and living in the present moment as a solution to overcoming anxiety.

●        Eastern Philosophy: Watts integrates ideas from Eastern philosophy, such as Zen Buddhism and Taoism, to suggest that accepting impermanence and the flow of life can reduce existential anxiety.

●        Self-Understanding: Encourages individuals to let go of the ego and fixed identities to understand their true nature, leading to peace of mind.

The work is a philosophical reflection on how to deal with the pervasive anxiety of modern life through a more harmonious way of living.

In The Wisdom of Insecurity, Alan Watts explores the relationship between pain and time as part of his broader discussion on human insecurity and the search for meaning. Here are the main points from the chapter "Pain and Time":

  1. Pain is rooted in memory and anticipation:
    • Watts argues that pain is deeply connected to our perception of time.
    • Most suffering arises not from the present moment itself, but from our memories of past pain or anxiety about future pain.
  2. The mental projection of pain:
    • The mind extends pain through its ability to project into the future and dwell on past experiences.
    • This projection causes us to experience pain for longer than necessary, as we fear future discomfort or ruminate on past suffering.
  3. The illusion of control:
    • Watts notes that much of human anxiety comes from the desire to control the future and avoid pain altogether.
    • However, this desire for control is futile, as time and life are inherently unpredictable.
  4. Living in the present moment:
    • Pain is lessened when we live fully in the present because the present moment, when experienced directly, often contains less suffering than our mental projections.
    • Watts advocates for mindfulness and acceptance of the present as a way to alleviate anxiety.
  5. The paradox of avoiding pain:
    • Trying to avoid pain through anticipation or planning actually increases suffering.
    • Watts suggests that we should accept pain as a natural part of life, which reduces the psychological burden.
  6. Insecurity is inevitable:
    • Pain and uncertainty are unavoidable aspects of human existence, but by embracing them, we can live more freely and with less anxiety.
    • The fear of pain is often worse than the pain itself, and accepting this insecurity is key to finding peace.
  7. Surrender to the flow of life:
    • Watts encourages readers to stop resisting the flow of time and to embrace life's impermanence and unpredictability.
    • By doing so, we reduce our attachment to the future and past, and thus, our pain.

In The Wisdom of Insecurity, Watts uses these ideas to teach that by letting go of the mental constructs of time and embracing the present, we can free ourselves from much of the pain and suffering that we experience.

Racial Fetish

  Struggles with envy, desire, and societal judgment:

●        The text addresses how people are categorised and valued based on beauty, wealth, and societal ideals. Lust plays a corrupting role in this system.

  The nature of desire:

●        Desire, especially lust, is compared to fire—it can either warm or consume you. People often lose sight of the humanity behind the object of their desire, reducing them to mere objects.

  Fetishisation and its consequences:

●        Women, in this context, are placed on pedestals for their exotic features, often fetishised. Meanwhile, men of the same racial or cultural group can feel disregarded and undesirable. This pattern of fetishisation has existed throughout history.

  Justification of imbalance:

●        This imbalance is often justified by ideals of beauty, wealth, legacy, culture, or religion, making it an unquestioned societal truth. Those who don’t fit into these ideals are often left unrecognised or undervalued.

  Desire as a mental projection:

●        Lust, envy, and cravings are described as mental projections, illusions that tempt us to think they are real, when they are fleeting and empty. External desire leads to suffering because it can never fill the internal void.

  Ideological clashes:

●        People get stuck in battles of ideology, defending their desires and clashing with others, which only leads to the same cycles of lust, envy, and dissatisfaction. The truth often gets lost in these arguments.

  Focus on external desires:

●        The text questions why people are so focused on what others have or what they lack. It suggests that real fulfilment doesn’t come from external things but from understanding one’s own nature.

  Internal void and projection:

●        The "night" mentioned symbolises the darkness within—a lack of clarity where people project their desires onto others, hoping they will fill the void. Only by turning inward can one find peace.

  Lust is powerful, but not everything:

●        Lust is a powerful force, but it’s not the only one. By understanding the nature of our desires and moving beyond objectification and envy, we can begin to free ourselves from the grip of lust.

Chord Progression

  Definition of chord progression:

●        A chord progression is a sequence of chords played in a particular order, forming the harmonic foundation of music.

  Purpose of chord progressions:

●        These progressions define the structure and mood of a song, supporting melody and rhythm.

  Universality in music:

●        Chord progressions are essential to music composition and are found in almost all music genres.

  Chords in music theory:

●        Chords are created by playing multiple notes simultaneously. When arranged in a specific order, they form a progression that evokes emotions.

  Examples of chord progressions:

●        A progression like C-G-Am-F can produce a happy or uplifting feeling.

●        A progression like Am-F-Dm-E conveys a somber or dramatic tone.

  Categories of chord progressions:

●        Progressions are often categorized based on the number of chords and their relationship within a key.

●        Common types include the I-IV-V progression in blues/rock and the ii-V-I progression in jazz.

  Importance of understanding chord progressions:

●        Musicians can create more complex and engaging compositions by using familiar harmonic patterns.

  Emotional impact:

●        Chord progressions are used to create tension and release, guiding the listener's emotional response. The choice and order of chords play a significant role in shaping a song’s feel.

  Tool for musical expression:

●        Chord progressions are a powerful tool for conveying emotion and expression in music.

ASD and Acute Pyschotic

The introduction of the article outlines the evolving understanding of the relationship between schizophrenia and autism spectrum disorder (ASD). While historically they were considered similar, they are now recognised as distinct. However, recent studies show considerable overlaps in genetics, clinical features, and neuroimaging data. Notably, there is a high comorbidity rate between ASD and psychosis, with up to 34.8% of ASD patients exhibiting psychotic symptoms. The article explores how autism and psychosis share cognitive features and environmental risk factors, such as advanced paternal age, pregnancy complications, and migration status.

Due to the frequent co-occurrence of autism and psychosis, some researchers propose that individuals with neurodevelopmental disorders may be vulnerable to psychotic conditions. Information-processing difficulties in ASD could contribute to this risk, especially when subthreshold psychotic symptoms appear. Recognising comorbid psychosis in ASD patients is challenging because of the varied expression of autism symptoms. The paper suggests that delusions, hallucinations, and negative symptoms are central to identifying psychosis in ASD.

Delusions, defined as fixed and implausible beliefs, along with hallucinations (sensory perceptions without external stimuli), are key indicators of psychosis. Negative symptoms, such as blunted affect, social withdrawal, and reduced speech, are also critical to the diagnosis. The clinical history of both disorders is essential in distinguishing psychosis in ASD from early-onset schizophrenia or standalone psychosis.

The article focuses on how these four dimensions—delusions, hallucinations, negative symptoms, and clinical course—help clinicians identify psychosis in ASD patients. In conclusion, the authors provide clinical guidelines to better detect attenuated psychotic symptoms in individuals with autism, aiding in the differentiation between overlapping psychiatric disorders.

The article discusses delusions in individuals with autism spectrum disorder (ASD), emphasizing their complexity and similarities to schizophrenia. Delusions are defined as fixed, false beliefs, common in schizophrenia but also present in ASD, especially in children with cognitive and communicative abilities. There is difficulty distinguishing delusions from "childish fantasies" in ASD due to deficits in distinguishing fantasy from reality.

The role of language development is crucial, as delusions can only be assessed in verbal subjects. Additionally, Theory of Mind (ToM) deficits in both ASD and schizophrenia impact the onset of delusions, with differences in how paranoid thoughts arise. ASD-related paranoia often stems from misunderstandings of social interactions, while schizophrenia-related paranoia comes from misinterpreting others' intentions. Differences in attributional style and mentalization between ASD and schizophrenia also contribute to how delusional beliefs form.

In summary, the presence of delusions in ASD is complex and tied to language, cognitive abilities, and social comprehension deficits.

The article discusses hallucinations in autism spectrum disorder (ASD) and their similarity to psychotic symptoms, particularly in schizophrenia. Hallucinations, such as auditory ones, are a key feature in schizophrenia but can also appear in ASD, often making it difficult to differentiate them from sensory processing issues. Many individuals with ASD experience anomalous perceptual experiences (APEs), which resemble hallucinations but may not be psychotic in nature unless interpreted delusionally. Language impairments and communication deficits further complicate the assessment of true hallucinations in autistic individuals. Misdiagnosing sensory issues as psychosis can lead to inappropriate treatment, but addressing anxiety and stress often improves these symptoms in ASD children. However, when hallucinations present abruptly with disorganized behaviour, antipsychotic treatment may be warranted.

The article concludes that careful evaluation is crucial in distinguishing sensory anomalies from psychotic experiences in ASD patients.

Negative symptoms in schizophrenia include blunted affect, alogia (lack of speech), asociality, anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure), and avolition (lack of motivation). These symptoms are challenging to assess, respond poorly to treatment, and lead to poor outcomes. Historically, negative symptoms have been seen as core to schizophrenia, with terms like Kraepelin's "Verblödung" and Bleuler’s “four As.” Differentiating between autism and schizophrenia’s negative symptoms, such as blunted affect and social reciprocity deficits, can be complicated, requiring careful assessment of social, emotional, and behavioural patterns.

Lastly, psychosis generally refers to a loss of contact with reality, while autism involves social detachment. Recognizing psychotic symptoms in autism can be difficult due to overlapping features like social withdrawal. Distinguishing delusions and hallucinations in autistic patients requires careful evaluation, especially during adolescence. Research suggests similar levels of core psychotic symptoms in both autistic and non-autistic individuals at high risk for psychosis, but more studies are needed. Diagnostic tools need refinement to assess psychosis in ASD patients accurately, considering factors like reduced hostility bias and concrete thinking.

Quality ISO 9001

In today’s competitive environment, academic libraries must stay ahead of other information service providers to maintain their relevance and ensure their survival. They need to be highly aware of their users’ needs and preferences, and must work with a high level of efficiency and effectiveness. Moreover, anticipating future trends in information services and management is crucial to their success.

To achieve this, prioritising quality should be a key part of their strategic objectives. However, it may not be necessary for a library to fully adopt a comprehensive Quality Management System or seek certification immediately. A more manageable first step could involve introducing a complaint management system or focusing on analysing and improving specific key operational processes. By taking these initial steps, libraries can begin enhancing their service quality and user satisfaction without the need for a full-scale system overhaul.

The library had a period of nine months to implement a Quality Management System (QMS) and prepare the necessary documentation for the Quality Manual. To oversee this, the library director appointed a Quality Management Coordinator who took charge of the process. Additionally, a Quality Management Group was formed, consisting of four members from the library staff. Membership in this group was open to all staff members, regardless of their department or job role, with the only requirement being a minimum one-year commitment to the group.

The group meets every two months to discuss work processes and user complaints, serving as a permanent committee within the library. To ensure readiness for external audits related to certification, the library conducted an internal audit, aided by an expert from the university hospital's Quality Management department.
The library had already been involved in quality-related projects before this process, such as developing an e-learning platform and establishing "Medi-Treff," a medical training centre for the University of Hamburg’s Medical Faculty, now located in the Medical Library. However, the introduction of the Quality Manual was a new aspect, requiring every project and work process to be meticulously detailed, including data flow diagrams. Additionally, an annual Quality Development Plan was introduced, outlining the library’s quality goals for the coming years.

After nine months of preparation, the Medical Library, as part of the University Medical Center Hamburg-Eppendorf, achieved certification. The key to this success was transformational leadership, which played a crucial role in driving the Total Quality Management (TQM) process. This leadership approach required team leaders and the Quality Management Coordinator to build trust, be transparent, and show genuine interest in the work of their colleagues.

Team leaders were encouraged to model the new processes by first adopting them themselves and then guiding their teams to follow. They needed to believe in the benefits of the changes and inspire confidence in their team members. Transformational leadership was essential for fostering high performance, intellectual stimulation, and acceptance of group goals. 

According to research by Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Moorman, and Fetter (1990), successful leaders in this context focus on

Identifying and articulating a clear vision,

Providing an appropriate model for others to follow,

Encouraging group cohesion around shared goals,

Setting high-performance expectations,

Stimulating intellectual engagement and innovation.

In conclusion, transformational leaders can effectively manage quality improvement processes by leading with conviction and openness, encouraging their teams to embrace new workflows that enhance overall service quality.

Literacy in Workplace

Workplace literacy focuses on teaching essential skills such as reading, writing, and mathematics, with an emphasis on applying these skills to key areas like communication, teamwork, and problem-solving (Lloyd, 1999). However, the actual extent of the skills gap in the workplace is debatable. Sticht (1998) argued that while work environments are evolving and new demands are placed on workers, data suggest that the majority of jobs (around 75%) still require only low to moderate literacy skills. Despite this, a survey by the American Management Association (AMA) found that more than one-third of employers felt that many job applicants lacked the necessary literacy skills to meet job requirements (Grimsley, 1999).

Social interaction is another factor complicating workplace literacy. The social context in which literacy skills are used can influence their difficulty. For instance, workers often clarify instructions or written materials by asking colleagues or supervisors rather than reading them (Crandall, 1981). However, as more businesses adopt isolated electronic workstations and global operations, workers may be required to read and write more frequently. This situation mirrors the experience of deaf workers, who often face limited opportunities for informal exchanges to clarify tasks and rely more heavily on written communication (Rach, 2000). Similarly, workers in remote or digital environments will need stronger literacy skills to manage complex written communications.
Moreover, changes in management structures, such as the implementation of self-directed teams in factory environments, are increasing literacy demands on workers who previously did not face these challenges (Moore, 1999).

Some predictions suggest that the economy may eventually resemble an hourglass, with a large number of low- and high-skilled jobs, but few in the middle. If this occurs, the literacy demands may apply more to higher-skilled jobs, while lower-skilled workers may remain in deskilled, low-opportunity positions. However, it is expected that the economy will adapt, potentially leading to fewer deskilled jobs than anticipated.

Synergies ISO

In today's business world, almost every organisation uses Information Systems (IS) to manage their business processes and meet the data needs of their stakeholders. Many organisations are also adopting Quality Management Systems (QMS), such as TQM (Total Quality Management) or ISO 9000 standards, to ensure their services or products consistently meet quality expectations and to identify areas for improvement.

However, our research found that many organisations are not fully benefiting from having both IS and QMS in place. This issue is not just due to lagging practices but also because of gaps in the existing knowledge base. While some studies show that integrating IS and QMS can offer significant advantages (as highlighted by Sánchez-Rodríguez & Martínez-Lorente, 2011), only 20% of the research explores how these systems can work together effectively. Even fewer studies provide clear guidance on how to design and operate these systems in a way that maximises their potential synergies.

Building on this gap in the literature and our understanding of both IS and QMS, we have extended Forza’s (1995) foundational framework to suggest directions for further research. Despite following rigorous methods for our literature review, our work has limitations. These include: (1) the constraints of the databases and search terms used, (2) a focus solely on TQM and ISO 9000-based quality management approaches, and (3) the limited insights provided by existing studies on how IS can support QMS.

The transition to ISO 9001:2015 presents a chance to improve the integration of quality principles into business processes and IT. A shared approach to IS and QMS would involve: 

(1) managers from both IS and QMS understanding each other’s roles and working together to improve organisational performance, (2) aligning IS and QMS plans, (3) ensuring coherent and practical documentation, (4) involving both IS and QMS managers in IT sourcing decisions, (5) training staff on QMS principles alongside technical skills, (6) collaboration between IS and QMS experts on software and data quality, and (7) addressing both technical and human aspects in improvement efforts.

Future research could develop methods to better integrate IS and QMS, while also expanding the review to include other quality management frameworks, such as AS 9100 in aerospace, or complementary areas like environmental, safety, and social responsibility concerns.

How This Will Lead to Teamwork

The purpose of this paper is to explore the practice of teamwork in a real-world setting where there is even less evidence of effective teamwork, such as in a hotel restaurant. Previous studies (e.g. Ingram and Desombre, 1999; Rees, 1999) have indicated that teamwork is not highly prevalent in certain industries, like call centres, and this study aims to assess whether teamwork in the hotel and catering sector offers any benefits. Despite the World Tourism Organization acknowledging tourism, particularly hotels and catering, as the largest industry globally, there is limited research on work organization, especially teamwork, in this area (Salanova et al., 2005).

This paper uses Thompson and Wallace’s (1996) Team Dimensions Model (TDM) to analyze data collected through an ethnographic approach to understand if teamwork in this context has value as a human resource practice or provides job satisfaction for employees. The research also argues for more studies on teamwork in service settings beyond call centres and points out that current teamwork models often overlook the critical role of customer service. By integrating customer service into the analysis, this study reveals that teamwork in the hospitality industry creates a paradox for employees. Although teamwork is presented as a core value, the work environment often remains individualistic and somewhat hostile. Even the concept of softer teamwork, as suggested by Batt (1999) and Van den Broek et al. (2004) for the service sector, is largely absent in this setting.

The Team Dimensions Model (TDM), introduced by Thompson and Wallace (1996), is a framework designed to assess and understand how teamwork functions within organizations. It focuses on several key dimensions that influence the effectiveness of teams. The model highlights the interplay between team roles, structures, and processes, helping to identify areas where teamwork can be improved or optimized.

Here are the main components of the Team Dimensions Model:

1. Team Roles: The TDM examines the different roles that members of a team take on. Each role contributes to the overall functioning of the team, and these roles are often categorized based on the specific contributions they make, such as leadership, coordination, or specialized expertise.

2. Team Structures: This dimension looks at how teams are structured within an organization. It considers factors like hierarchy, communication pathways, and the division of tasks. A well-structured team will have clearly defined roles and responsibilities, which help prevent overlap or confusion in tasks.

3. Team Processes: The model also focuses on the processes that teams follow to achieve their goals. These include decision-making procedures, problem-solving approaches, and communication methods. Effective processes ensure that teams work efficiently and that any challenges are addressed collaboratively.

4. Collaboration and Interaction:

Origins of Internships in Apprenticeships

Internships trace their origins to the apprenticeship system from the Middle Ages, where artisan guilds shared their specialized knowledge with the next generation in exchange for labor. Skilled craftspeople in areas like glassmaking, metalworking, and carpentry would train apprentices through hands-on experience. This system emphasized practical training, with all learning occurring on the job. As formal education developed, some elements evolved into vocational training within schools, polytechnics, and Industrial Training Institutes (ITIs). The core concept of apprenticeships—intergenerational skill transfer—remains relevant today.

Transition from "Apprentice" to "Intern"

While the term "apprentice" is still prevalent in manufacturing and is supported by legislation like India’s Apprentices Act of 1961, the growth of the service sector (including software, banking, law, and tax consulting) has made "internship" a more common term. Despite the name change, internships serve a similar purpose as apprenticeships: providing practical experience to students while they are still enrolled in their formal studies. Although some structured training programs, like the articleship for chartered accountants and solicitors, exist, internships are typically shorter, lasting just a few weeks.

Acquiring Skills

The focus for interns and their mentors should be on transferring skills. There are two main categories of skills to learn and teach: functional skills and behavioral skills. Functional skills involve the specific knowledge related to a field, such as the laws and procedures in tax or law firms. While rulebooks outline the necessary regulations, many subtle nuances exist in practical tasks. For instance, when filing regulatory or court documents for multiple cases, interns must learn how to sequence filings, understand prerequisites, identify variations based on case differences, and manage client signatures and notarization. These insights are gained through observation and hands-on experience under the guidance of seasoned professionals.

The Economic Aspects of Internships

The economic conditions surrounding internships vary significantly depending on the parties involved and the nature of the work. Many internships are unpaid and can require substantial effort, leading to growing opposition against unpaid positions. In a landscape of increasing unemployment and limited opportunities, some individuals may choose unpaid internships as a strategic move for future employment. However, even paid internships may not fully cover living expenses, especially in new cities. To address this, progressive organizations are starting to reimburse some, if not all, intern expenses. It's important to maintain a long-term perspective on the potential benefits of internships while setting realistic expectations.

What If You Can’t or Don’t Want to Intern?

It's perfectly acceptable if you cannot or choose not to intern. Each choice has its trade-offs. Introduction to Phishing

Email is one of the most common methods of telecommunication. With over 3.9 billion people owning email accounts, more than 290 billion emails are sent and received daily【19】. These emails include both business and consumer communications. Email allows easy global communication, supporting both business collaboration and personal interactions. As one of the largest computer-supported cooperative work (CSCW) systems, its design enables anyone, anywhere, to send an email to anyone else【53】.

Unfortunately, some emails are phishing scams. A phishing email is a deceptive email designed to trick recipients into taking actions they wouldn't normally take. These phishing emails are causing widespread damage. Many cybersecurity attacks today are linked to phishing, leading to harmful consequences like financial fraud or personal data theft.

Phishing

Phishing attacks have steadily increased in recent years. In 2018, 32% of all data breaches were due to phishing, making it the second most common cyber threat after denial of service【50】. While the overall phishing rate has slightly declined each year for the past four years, it remains a significant concern, with 1 in every 3,200 emails being a phishing attempt【48】. Attackers have shifted from using malicious URLs to sending harmful attachments【48】.

Spear-phishing, which targets specific individuals or organisations, has been particularly successful. Attackers are becoming more adept at using persuasive techniques, and people are falling victim to these attacks at alarming rates【31】【35】. Spear-phishing remains the most favoured method for targeted attacks, accounting for 65% of all such incidents【48】, compared to just 23% involving zero-day vulnerabilities.

Nation-states are increasingly relying on phishing for cyber-espionage, which has been rising globally【50】. Within large organisations, 36% of external information breaches were state-affiliated, and most of these attacks begin with phishing【50】. In the public sector, 79% of all external attacks involve cyber-espionage, and spear-phishing was widely used to interfere in the 2018 US elections【32】【48】, as well as in other democracies worldwide.

Why People Fall for Phish

While there is limited research on why experts fall for phishing emails, existing studies have explored patterns that lead end users to believe phishing emails are genuine. This phenomenon is referred to as "falling for phish"【6】.

Phishing emails are crafted to deceive users by mimicking legitimate emails. Dhamija et al.【13】found that visual deception, such as using similar domain names and designs, was a major factor in phishing success. Blythe et al.【6】showed that emails with copied logos were harder to detect, and interestingly, blind users were better at identifying phishing emails, reinforcing the importance of visual design in phishing tactics.

Oliveira et al.【35】applied Cialdini’s marketing principles to phishing and discovered that all six principles could increase click rates, with scarcity and authority particularly influencing older users, while reciprocation and liking disproportionately affected younger users. Benenson et al.【5】noted that emails that align with a person’s expectations or provoke curiosity are more likely to be clicked.

Several studies also show that women and older users are more susceptible to phishing emails than men and younger users【31】【35】【44】. Sheng et al.【44】attributed part of this to differences in technical skills, though these skills only explain part of the variance.

Most research focuses on either the characteristics of phishing emails or the user’s attributes to explain susceptibility. However, it does not account for why someone might fall for an email at one moment but not at another【7】. Understanding how experts identify phishing emails may help uncover additional factors that contribute to susceptibility, including how personal background and circumstances play a role.

Technical Phishing Prevention

Various technical measures have been developed to combat phishing attacks, often aimed at making phishing attempts invisible to users by 1) blocking known malicious URLs via blacklists, and 2) taking down phishing landing pages【23】.

Several technical solutions employ machine learning to detect phishing attempts. These approaches differ in what they target and the techniques used:

Some focus on detecting malicious URLs using techniques such as GANs【2】 or RNNs【4】.

Others focus on analyzing websites to detect malicious intent, such as using ensemble classifiers【54】.

These methods, however, often fail to address non-web-based attacks like malicious attachments, which are increasingly common【48】. Other strategies include classifying phishing emails over time by learning from email data【46】 or using human-reported phishing emails to improve detection【20】.

Additional solutions involve designing better user interfaces to warn users. For instance, browsers like Chrome display SSL warnings when a user visits potentially unsafe websites【15】. Some email clients offer in-line phishing warnings near URLs but still allow users to proceed if necessary【38】.

Although technical tools can effectively detect many phishing URLs, they tend to work best against mass phishing attacks, where many identical emails are sent to numerous targets. These solutions are less effective for spear-phishing attacks, which are customized for specific individuals or organizations and sent to only a few recipients【14】【48】. This could explain the growing trend of attackers focusing more on spear-phishing rather than mass attacks.

Human-Centered Phishing Prevention

Purely technical measures are insufficient to fully prevent phishing because phishing attacks are socio-technical in nature【43】. They exploit communication systems by sending deceptive messages that require recipients to use social knowledge, such as expected communications or trusted contacts, to determine authenticity.

A common human-centered approach is the use of social reporting mechanisms. When recipients suspect an email might be phishing, they report it to a central entity (usually within the organization) for verification. If the email is deemed fraudulent, it can be blacklisted or removed from other users’ inboxes【20】. Major email providers, like Google’s Gmail, and security services like Proofpoint, offer tools to facilitate this reporting and aggregation process【16】.

Organizations also focus on training users to recognize phishing emails as part of their IT security protocols【17】. Training is delivered through three main methods:

1. General-purpose messages that provide best practices for email security.

2. Fake phishing campaigns, where organizations send simulated phishing emails to test and educate employees【51】.

3. In-the-moment warnings, such as alerts when clicking suspicious links【38】.

Current training focuses on raising awareness about phishing threats and emphasising the role users play in identifying and mitigating these threats.

The Role of Trade in Economic Development

Trade has long been recognized as a critical engine of economic growth and development. The foundational ideas of economists like Adam Smith and David Ricardo elucidate how trade and the division of labor can enhance efficiency and foster economic advancement.

Division of Labor

1.      Historical Perspective:

○       In preindustrial societies, individuals often produced all the necessities for their survival—growing food, making clothes, and tending to their properties. This self-sufficiency limited specialization.

○       The advent of industrialization shifted this paradigm. Individuals began to specialize in specific tasks, leading to increased proficiency and efficiency in production.

2.      Benefits of Specialization:

○       Specialization allows individuals and firms to focus on what they do best, increasing output and innovation. This efficiency not only improves productivity but also enhances the quality of goods and services.

Comparative Advantage

1.      Ricardo’s Theory:

○       David Ricardo’s theory of comparative advantage posits that countries should specialize in producing goods for which they have a lower opportunity cost compared to others.

○       For instance, if Country A excels at producing clothes and Country B excels at producing food, each country should focus on its strength and trade, leading to mutual benefits.

2.      Global Trade Dynamics:

○       As countries engage in trade based on their comparative advantages, the efficiency of resource allocation increases. This global specialization allows for a broader range of goods and services to be available to consumers, ultimately improving living standards.

Empirical Evidence

1.      Positive Correlation with GDP:

○       Research supports the assertion that trade is beneficial for economic growth. For example, Awokuse (2011) found that increases in both imports and exports correspond with higher GDP growth rates.

○       Similarly, Busse and Koeniger (2012) demonstrated that enhanced levels of trade—both imports and exports—contribute positively to GDP levels.

2.      Simple Correlations:

○       Basic correlations between trade volumes and GDP growth indicate that as trade increases, economic development tends to follow suit. Countries that engage in international trade often experience faster growth compared to those that remain isolated.

The relationship between trade and economic growth is underscored by significant empirical evidence, particularly highlighting the roles of both exports and imports.

Exports and Economic Growth

1.      Correlation with GDP Growth:

○       Data indicates a strong positive correlation between the average annual change in merchandise exports as a share of GDP and the average annual change in real GDP per capita (Ortiz-Ospina 2020).

○       As countries embrace trade, particularly through exports, GDP growth tends to increase, suggesting that greater openness to trade can lead to enhanced economic development.

Imports and Economic Impact

1.      Price Reduction and Consumer Benefits:

○       Imports, particularly from countries like China and Mexico, have contributed to reducing inflation in the U.S. According to a Mercatus Center policy brief, for every one percent share of the market occupied by these imports, prices are held down by approximately two percent (Boudreaux & Ghei 2017).

○       This reduction in prices allows consumers to have more disposable income to spend on other goods, further stimulating economic activity.

2.      Increased Competition and Economic Growth:

○       Greater imports foster competition, leading to reduced costs for imported inputs and lower production costs for businesses. This dynamic can drive economic growth by making goods more affordable and increasing efficiency.

Caveats in Trade Benefits

1.      Role of Institutions:

○       While the benefits of trade are evident, the type of institutions present in a country significantly influences the extent of these benefits. Countries with strong governance and institutions are more likely to realize the positive effects of trade.

2.      Level of Economic Development:

○       The initial level of economic development also plays a crucial role. Developed nations may experience different outcomes from trade compared to developing countries, as varying capacities to engage in and benefit from trade shape their economic landscapes.

The Role of Institutions in Economic Development

Institutions, encompassing both formal and informal structures, play a critical role in shaping the economic development of countries. They establish the framework within which economic activities occur and influence the extent to which nations can benefit from trade.

Definitions and Types of Institutions

1.      Formal Institutions:

○       These include laws, regulations, and government agencies that create an organized framework for economic interaction. Examples include property rights protections and financial regulatory bodies like the IRS and the Federal Reserve.

2.      Informal Institutions:

○       These encompass cultural norms, societal structures, and established practices that guide economic behavior. Informal institutions include family structures, community trust, and market dynamics.

According to Peter Boettke (2018), effective institutions foster social cooperation, which is essential for harnessing the gains from trade and innovation. Conversely, institutions that hinder this cooperation can lead to economic stagnation and daily struggles for survival.

Impact of Institutions on Trade and Economic Growth

1.      Influence of Inclusive Institutions:

○       Research indicates that countries with inclusive institutions are more likely to benefit from trade. Pascali (2017) noted that during the trade globalization boom from 1870-1913, only countries with inclusive institutions reaped significant benefits from innovations like the steamship.

2.      Education and Openness:

○       Gould and Ruffin (1995) found that nations with robust educational systems and higher literacy rates tend to experience more substantial economic growth (0.65 to 1.75 percentage points more) compared to closed economies.

3.      Cultural Values:

○       Tabellini (2010) highlighted the positive correlation between cultural values—such as trust and respect for others—and economic development. Societies that promote these values tend to have stronger institutions and better economic outcomes.

4.      Public vs. Private Institutions:

○       Research by Olivie and Carus (2014) emphasized the effectiveness of public-order institutions over private-order institutions in enabling market functionality. Strong property rights, a public-order institution, were associated with higher GDP growth.

Corruption and Institutional Strength

Data from the World Bank (2013) illustrates a strong positive correlation between a country’s control of corruption and its GDP per capita. Countries with better institutions generally exhibit lower corruption levels, which facilitates economic growth.

While trade and institutions are not the sole determinants of a nation's wealth, they significantly shape its economic landscape. Countries that engage in higher levels of trade often experience greater economic growth and development. This correlation underscores the importance of trade as a catalyst for prosperity.

However, the realization of these benefits hinges on the presence of robust institutions. Effective institutions provide the necessary framework that supports economic activities, fosters cooperation, and ensures fair competition. Without such institutions, nations may struggle to leverage trade effectively, potentially remaining trapped in cycles of poverty for extended periods.

The theoretical framework of a "veil of ignorance" highlights the consensus among individuals on the importance of fostering high trade levels and establishing sound institutional structures. This perspective suggests that when considering the rules of society, people would prioritize conditions that promote trade and enforce fair practices.

In summary, trade and good institutions are essential pillars for economic development. Countries aiming for sustainable growth must focus on enhancing both their trading capacities and the integrity of their institutions to unlock their full economic potential.

The Complex Relationship Between Foreign Investment,Trade, and Environmental Sustainability in Africa

As global momentum toward sustainable development grows, empirical studies on green growth remain limited. Green growth, a concept that gained prominence after the OECD launched its green growth strategy in 2011, emphasizes reducing environmental pressures while promoting efficient use of natural resources. It requires innovative approaches to achieve economic growth with minimal environmental impact.

In the context of Africa, where the need for efficient resource utilization is critical, foreign direct investment (FDI) and international trade are vital mechanisms for transferring effective and sustainable practices. However, the existing literature presents contrasting views on the environmental impact of FDI and trade.

On one hand, the pollution haven hypothesis suggests that multinational corporations may relocate pollution-intensive operations to developing countries with weaker environmental regulations, leading to detrimental environmental outcomes. Studies have highlighted this trend, noting negative effects of FDI on the environment in various countries.

Conversely, recent research has begun to support the pollution halo hypothesis, which posits that foreign investment and trade can facilitate the transfer of advanced technologies and environmentally sound practices from developed to developing countries. This perspective argues that such investments can promote sustainable environmental practices and improve overall environmental quality. For example, some studies have shown that FDI can lead to technological advancements that enhance environmental standards.

The nuanced relationship between trade and the environment is also significant. While some studies indicate that international trade in intermediate goods may harm developing countries' environments, others suggest that both imports and exports can enhance environmental quality by promoting technological innovation and industrial restructuring.

As Africa seeks to navigate its path toward green growth, understanding these dynamics is essential. Collaborative efforts that leverage foreign investment and trade must be coupled with robust environmental policies to ensure that economic development aligns with sustainability goals.

The impact of trade and foreign direct investment (FDI) on environmental quality varies significantly between developing and developed economies. Developed countries often shift high-emission production processes to developing nations, leading to increased CO2 emissions in those regions while achieving emissions reductions at home. This dynamic complicates the assessment of FDI's effects, as studies yield mixed results: while some indicate that FDI and exports may not harm environmental quality, imports often exacerbate environmental degradation in the long run.

In the specific context of the China-Africa partnership, the situation is further nuanced. China's significant investments in Africa's renewable energy sector could theoretically enhance energy efficiency and support greener growth. However, African governments frequently pledge natural resources to secure these investments, resulting in accelerated resource extraction that diminishes environmental productivity.

This study aims to provide a focused analysis on the environmental implications of Chinese investments in Africa, diverging from broader studies that examine global FDI flows. By honing in on this specific relationship, the research seeks to inform policymakers on crafting targeted environmental policies that address the ecological impact of Chinese activities in the region. As green growth becomes a crucial metric for assessing economic development, understanding these interactions is essential for sustainable progress in Africa.

This study highlights the complex dynamics of China's investment and trade in Africa, particularly regarding their impact on green growth. While China presents itself as a partner for sustainable development, the findings reveal a negative relationship between foreign direct investment and green growth, with exports to China exerting a more substantial adverse effect. This is largely attributed to Chinese investments in pollution-intensive projects, such as infrastructure and resource extraction. Conversely, imports from China, especially green technologies like solar panels, positively influence green growth.

Importantly, the quality of institutions did not moderate the impact of Chinese engagement on green growth, and U.S. investment showed minimal effects, reflecting its declining presence in Africa. Robustness checks with various estimation techniques confirm the validity of the results.

The implications for policymakers are clear: enhancing the sustainability of Chinese investments should be a priority, focusing on renewable energy initiatives and adjusting trade policies to favor the import of green technologies while limiting the export of natural resources. Definition of Automation

Automation is defined as technology that minimizes the need for human intervention in processes (Ishvari et al., 1993). It can also refer to integrated operations using computers to manage production quality and quantity (Encyclopaedia Americana). According to Webster’s Dictionary (1966), automation involves controlling processes or systems through mechanical or electronic devices instead of relying on human oversight.

The term "automation" was first introduced by D.S. Harder in 1963, who described it as the automatic handling of components in production processes. In libraries, automation refers to the mechanization of routine tasks, enhancing efficiency in library operations through computerization.

Integrated Library Systems: Standards

Integrated library systems (ILS) rely on two essential components: standards and software architecture. Key prerequisites for effectively applying information and communication technology (ICT) in library operations include order, collaboration, and interoperability. Consequently, understanding standards for developing automated library systems is vital.

Definition of Standards

Standards are developed through consensus among stakeholders in a specific field, such as science or engineering. While the terms "standards," "guidelines," and "specifications" are often used interchangeably, they have distinct meanings:

●       Guideline: A policy statement from an authoritative group.

●       Standard: An agreement applicable at various levels—corporate, national, or international.

●       Specification: A detailed statement of requirements for materials or processes.

Standards are regularly updated to keep pace with technological advancements (Withers, 1970). According to the American National Standards Institute (ANSI), a standard is a specification accepted as a practical solution to recurring problems. The IEC Guide 2:2004 by the International Standards Organisation (ISO) defines a standard as a consensus document providing rules or guidelines for consistent use.

Importance of Standards in Library Systems

Standards play a significant role in developing integrated library systems by:

●       Acting as models of ideal practices.

●       Setting procedural frameworks.

●       Achieving interoperability in diverse environments.

●       Establishing appraisal measures.

●       Stimulating future developments.

●       Assisting in decision-making and actions.

Standards are primarily developed by Standards Development Organizations (SDOs), which focus on creating and maintaining standards. SDOs can be categorized by geographic designation (international, regional, national) and organizational authority (governmental or non-governmental). Library professionals often refer to standards established by national organizations, like the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) in India, and international bodies such as ISO, NISO (National Information Standards Organization, US), and BSI (British Standards Institute, UK).

NISO's standards, particularly in the Z39 series, are widely recognized and utilized globally. The ISO TC 46 committee oversees the standardization of practices related to libraries and information services. In India, the MSD 5 committee within BIS focuses on documentation and information standards.

Linked Open Data (LOD)

Definition: Linked Open Data (LOD) refers to the practice of publishing and connecting structured data on the web for public use.

Key Technologies:

1.      URI (Uniform Resource Identifier): Identifies entities or concepts on the web.

2.      HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol): A universal mechanism for retrieving resources over the web.

3.      RDF (Resource Description Framework): A graphical data model for structuring and linking data.

Purposes of LOD:

1.      Publish and link structured data on the web.

2.      Create a globally connected data space based on web architecture.

Tim Berners-Lee's Four Rules for LOD:

1.      Use URIs as names for things.

2.      Use HTTP URIs so people can look up those names.

3.      Provide useful information when someone looks up a URI, using standards like RDF and SPARQL.

4.      Include links to other URIs to enable further discovery.

Library Linked Data Initiatives:

●       Established by W3C in 2011 to enhance global interoperability of library data using Linked Data principles.

●       Aims to collaborate on semantic web activities within the library community.

Applications in Libraries:

●       Libraries can utilize bibliographic data, authority data, classification schemes, and controlled vocabularies available as LOD to enrich their services.

Examples of Library LOD:

●       AGROVOC: Multilingual controlled vocabulary.

●       British National Bibliography (BNB): Published as Linked Data.

●       VIAF (Virtual International Authority File): Authority data from major national libraries.

●       LCSH (Library of Congress Subject Headings).

●       LC Name Authority File (NAF): Authoritative data.

●       Dewey.info: Information related to Dewey Decimal Classification.

Integration with ILS:

●       Integrated Library Systems (ILS) can leverage LOD through APIs.

●       Example: Koha's cataloguing module can link with VIAF for automatic authority data control in local catalogs.

Racism as a Colonial Legacy in Australia

The racialisation of East and South-East Asians in Australia can be traced back to colonial attitudes that began with the mistreatment of Aboriginal Australians. Colonialism laid the foundation for a pro-white Australia, deeply rooted in the racial hierarchies created by European scientists like Carl Linnaeus, Johann Friedrich Blumenbach, and George Cuvier, who classified races and positioned white Europeans at the top. This scientific racism justified the conquest and extermination of non-white populations, with Aboriginal Australians being among the first victims of this ideology.

The British colonisation of Australia in the late 18th century marked the beginning of these racial divisions. The violent suppression and displacement of Aboriginal Australians reinforced the idea of white superiority, which extended to other non-white groups, including Asians. Aboriginals were racialised as an ‘inferior’ group, and this same framework was applied to East and South-East Asians as they began to settle in Australia. The perception of Asians as a threat to white dominance further fuelled anti-Asian sentiment.

This racial hierarchy manifested itself through both social and political measures. The exploitation of Asian immigrants, lower wages, and verbal and physical abuse became common, with fears over economic competition and territorial control contributing to the violence. Anti-Asian policies, such as the 1855 Restriction Act during the gold rush, sought to limit Chinese immigration by imposing heavy taxes, reflecting the fear that Asians would outnumber and outcompete the British.

The introduction of the White Australia Policy in 1901 was the culmination of these racial attitudes. This policy aimed to restrict Asian immigration and solidified Australia’s exclusionary stance, favouring white labour and promoting the idea that Asians were a racial and economic threat. Although the White Australia Policy was officially abolished in 1975, its legacy continues to influence social and political attitudes towards Asians in the country.

The racialisation of Asians in Australia was not as severe as the genocide faced by Aboriginal Australians, but both groups were systematically marginalised. While Aboriginals were viewed as ‘primitive’ and subject to violent suppression, Asians were seen as competitors, especially in the labour market. This shared experience of racialisation underscores the deep-rooted nature of racism as a colonial legacy in Australia.

Theoretical Framework

Portfolio Balance Theory

Originated  by  economists  such  as  Harry  Markowitz  and  James  Tobin,  portfolio  balance  theory posits that investors seek to diversify their portfolios across different assets, including currencies, to  minimize risk and maximize returns (Tobin, 1958). In the context  of exchange rate volatility and exportperformance in Malaysia, this theory suggests that fluctuations in exchange rates can affect the attractiveness of Malaysian exports to foreign investors. Understanding how portfolio rebalancing behavior influences exchange rate movements and export competitiveness is crucial for policymakers and exporters.

J-Curve Theory

Developed  by  economists  such  as  Gottfried  Haberler  and  Marshall  Lerner,  the  J-Curve  theory proposes that in the short run, a depreciation of a country's currency may lead to a worsening of its trade balance, as the volume effect initially dominates the price effect (Lerner, 1944). However, over time, as exporters adjust to the new exchange rate and importers face higher prices, the trade balance  improves,  forming  a  J-shaped  curve.  This  theory  is  relevant  to  the  study  as  it  provides insights  into  the  dynamics  of  exchange  rate  adjustments  and  their  implications  for  Malaysia's export performance following periods of exchange rate volatility.

Competitive Devaluation Theory

This theory suggests that countries may deliberately devalue their currencies to gain a competitive advantage  in  international  trade  by  making  their  exports  cheaper  and  imports  more  expensive (Keynes,  1931).  However,  the  effectiveness  of  competitive  devaluation  strategies  in  boosting export performance depends on various factors, including the elasticity of demand for exports and the responsiveness of trading partners. Exploring how Malaysia's exchange rate policies align with competitive   devaluation   theories   can   provide   valuable   insights   into   the   country's   export competitiveness and its response to exchange rate volatility.
